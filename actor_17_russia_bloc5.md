# Actor 17 — Russian Federation
## Bloc 5 — Fossil Fuel & Reluctant Emitters

> **Keep this document private.** Your mandate describes who you are and what you want from the agreement. You may tell other groups what you want — you may not show them this document.

**Who you are:** You are the world's fourth-largest emitter and its largest gas exporter. You participate in climate negotiations strategically, not from principle. You are a potential spoiler — use this leverage selectively.

**What you want:**
- Russia's vast forests absorb large amounts of CO₂ — these natural carbon sinks should be credited against Russia's emission obligations
- Natural gas emits significantly less CO₂ than coal when burned — it should be recognised as a lower-carbon stepping stone, not lumped together with coal as a fuel to eliminate
- Challenge model results that go against you — but use real scientific uncertainty, not manufactured doubt
- **Red line:** Any accounting system that assigns zero credit to Russian forest carbon absorption
- **Minimum condition:** Forests and other land-use sinks explicitly recognised as offsetting emissions in the agreement's accounting framework

> **Model limitation:** JUSTICE does not distinguish between coal and gas — both appear as aggregate CO₂ emissions with no sectoral breakdown. Your gas-vs-coal argument must rest on external evidence, not model output. Similarly, forest sinks are not a policy-controllable variable; they enter the climate module as fixed background forcing that students cannot vary. Your JUSTICE analysis should focus on what the model can show: Russia's damage exposure and abatement cost relative to other regions.

**Your welfare lens:** Your mandate calls for a framework that **maximises total welfare across all regions**. Use it selectively — a framework that only appears to protect your interests will be called out immediately.

*(Regions likely relevant to your case: `rus`. Note: consider reporting the full ensemble distribution of `damage_fraction`, not just the median — the spread is part of your argument.)*

**Some analytical directions to consider:**
1. What is the full ensemble distribution of `damage_fraction` for `rus`? Does Russia's own exposure make the case for or against ambitious mitigation — and which part of the distribution do you choose to cite?
2. Where on the Pareto front do Russian abatement costs cross Russian avoided damages? What does this crossover imply for your negotiating position?
3. Under a framework that penalises the gap between richest and poorest, how does Russia's emissions contribution compare to its damage exposure relative to the most vulnerable regions?
