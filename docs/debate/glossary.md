# Glossary of Climate Negotiation Terms

This glossary defines the technical and political language used in climate negotiations. All actors should be familiar with these terms before the debate.

---

### The Climate Problem

**Mitigation** — Actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions or increase carbon sinks, addressing the root cause of climate change. Examples: switching from coal to renewables, improving energy efficiency, stopping deforestation.

**Adaptation** — Actions that reduce the harm caused by climate change that is already happening or locked in. Examples: building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops, relocating coastal communities. Adaptation does not reduce emissions — it manages consequences.

**Emissions gap** — The difference between what countries have pledged to do and what science says is needed to meet the temperature targets of the Paris Agreement. As of 2025, the gap remains large.

**Net zero** — A state in which the greenhouse gases emitted into the atmosphere are balanced by an equal amount being removed. Reaching net zero does not mean zero emissions — it means any remaining emissions are offset by carbon removal.

**Overshoot** — A scenario in which global temperature temporarily exceeds a target (e.g. 1.5°C) before being brought back down, typically through large-scale carbon removal later in the century. Controversial because the damage done during the overshoot period is real and irreversible for the most vulnerable.

**1.5°C / 2°C targets** — The temperature thresholds established by the Paris Agreement. 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels is the more ambitious target, demanded by the most vulnerable countries. 2°C is the fallback. Beyond these thresholds, climate science projects increasingly severe and potentially irreversible impacts.

**Carbon sink** — A natural or artificial system that absorbs more CO₂ from the atmosphere than it releases. Forests, oceans, and soils are natural sinks. Russia and Brazil use their forest sinks as a key argument in negotiations.

**Stranded assets** — Fossil fuel infrastructure (coal plants, oil fields, gas pipelines) that would lose economic value before the end of its expected lifetime if climate policy tightens. A major source of political resistance to rapid phase-out.

### Finance

**Climate finance** — Money that flows from wealthier countries to developing countries to help them reduce emissions and adapt to climate impacts. The original pledge of $100 billion per year by 2020 (made in 2009) was not met on time. At COP29, a new goal (NCQG) was set.

**Adaptation finance** — Climate finance specifically directed at helping countries cope with the impacts of climate change that are already happening — building resilience, not reducing emissions.

**Loss and Damage** — Compensation for climate harms that go beyond what adaptation can address — permanent losses such as submerged land, destroyed ecosystems, forced migration, and cultural heritage that cannot be recovered. Distinct from adaptation finance. A separate Loss and Damage fund was established at COP27.

**Grants vs loans** — The form in which climate finance is delivered. Grants are non-repayable. Loans must be repaid with interest. Vulnerable and heavily indebted developing countries insist on grants; many developed countries prefer loans as they leverage more capital. This distinction is one of the sharpest dividing lines in climate finance negotiations.

**Blended finance** — The use of public or philanthropic money to attract private investment into climate projects by reducing the risk for private investors. Effective in middle-income countries; less effective in the poorest countries where private capital does not flow regardless.

**New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG)** — The successor to the $100 billion climate finance pledge, agreed at COP29. Sets a new annual climate finance target from developed to developing countries.

**Just Transition fund** — A dedicated fund to support workers and communities in fossil fuel-dependent industries and regions as those industries decline. Distinct from adaptation or mitigation finance — it addresses the social and economic costs of the transition itself.

**Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP)** — A bilateral or multilateral financing arrangement in which developed countries provide a package of grants, loans, and investment to help a specific coal-dependent country accelerate its energy transition. South Africa, Indonesia, and Vietnam have JETPs.

**Additionality** — The principle that climate finance must be new money, not redirected from existing aid or development budgets. A frequent source of dispute — developing countries argue that much of what is counted as climate finance is relabelled aid.

### Fossil Fuels and Technology

**Phase-out** — A commitment to end the use of a fuel or technology entirely, with a defined timeline. The strongest language in negotiations.

**Phase-down** — A commitment to gradually reduce the use of a fuel or technology, without a fixed end date or endpoint. Weaker than phase-out. The COP28 agreement referred to "transitioning away from fossil fuels" — a compromise between those demanding phase-out and those resisting any such language.

**Unabated fossil fuels** — Fossil fuels burned without technology to capture the resulting CO₂. "Phase-down of unabated fossil fuels" is the language preferred by fossil fuel producers — it allows continued use if paired with carbon capture, even if capture is not yet deployed at scale.

**Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)** — Technology that captures CO₂ at the point of emission (e.g. from a power plant or industrial facility) and stores it underground. Technically proven at small scale; contested because it is expensive, energy-intensive, and has not been deployed at the scale needed to justify continued fossil fuel use. Used by fossil fuel producers to argue for technology neutrality.

**Direct Air Carbon Capture (DACCS)** — Technology that captures CO₂ directly from the ambient air, not from a point source. Even more expensive and energy-intensive than CCS. Often referenced in overshoot scenarios as the mechanism by which temperatures would be brought back down after exceeding a target.

**Blue hydrogen** — Hydrogen produced from natural gas, with the resulting CO₂ captured and stored. Marketed as a low-carbon fuel. Critics argue that methane leakage in the supply chain makes its climate benefit uncertain.

**Green hydrogen** — Hydrogen produced by electrolysis using renewable electricity. Genuinely low-carbon but currently expensive.

**Ammonia (as fuel)** — A hydrogen carrier that can be co-fired in coal power plants to reduce emissions. Japan and South Korea promote this as a pathway to decarbonise power generation without decommissioning existing plants. Critics call it a delay tactic.

**Technology neutrality** — The principle that an agreement should set emission targets but not specify which technologies countries must use to meet them. Favoured by Japan, South Korea, and fossil fuel producers because it keeps CCS, hydrogen, and ammonia on the table. Opposed by those who see it as a way to avoid committing to renewables.

**Energy poverty** — Lack of access to reliable, affordable, modern energy. Used as an argument against rapid fossil fuel phase-out in developing countries — particularly India, Indonesia, and parts of Africa — where hundreds of millions still rely on solid fuels for cooking and heating.

**Fossil fuel subsidies** — Government support (direct payments, tax breaks, underpriced energy) that makes fossil fuels cheaper than they would otherwise be. The IEA estimates global fossil fuel subsidies at over $1 trillion per year. Eliminating them is frequently discussed but rarely acted upon.

### Negotiating Concepts

**Paris Agreement** — The 2015 international treaty under the UNFCCC in which countries committed to limit global warming to well below 2°C, pursuing efforts toward 1.5°C. Operates on voluntary nationally determined contributions (NDCs), not binding emission targets.

**Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)** — Each country's self-declared climate pledge under the Paris Agreement — what it commits to do on emissions, adaptation, and finance. NDCs are not legally binding in their ambition, only in their submission.

**Ambition ratchet** — The mechanism in the Paris Agreement requiring countries to submit increasingly ambitious NDCs every five years. The idea is that ambition increases over time even if the initial pledges are insufficient. In practice, the ratchet has not produced adequate ambition.

**Common but Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capabilities (CBDR-RC)** — The foundational equity principle of the UNFCCC: all countries share responsibility for addressing climate change, but the degree of responsibility and required action differs based on historical emissions and economic capacity. Developed countries bear greater responsibility. Used by China, India, and the G77 to resist equal obligations.

**Differentiation** — In practice, the application of CBDR-RC to specific policy commitments — the idea that different countries should face different timelines, targets, or obligations based on their development level, capacity, and historical responsibility. The line between differentiation and unfair exemption is constantly contested.

**Historical responsibility** — The principle that countries that emitted greenhouse gases during industrialisation (primarily wealthy Western nations) bear special responsibility for the resulting climate damage, even if their current emissions are lower than emerging economies. Central to the arguments of China, India, and the G77.

**Equity** — The broad principle that the burdens and benefits of climate action should be distributed fairly — taking into account historical responsibility, capacity to act, and vulnerability to impacts. Different actors operationalise equity very differently.

**Red line** — A position that an actor will not cross under any circumstances — a condition whose breach would cause them to vote against or walk away from an agreement.

**Minimum condition for yes** — The least an actor needs in the final agreement to cast a yes vote. Distinct from their ideal position. Identifying minimum conditions is how the UNFCCC Chair and Turkey map the Zone of Possible Agreement.

**Zone of Possible Agreement (ZOPA)** — The space in which a deal is possible — the set of agreement texts that satisfy enough blocs' minimum conditions simultaneously. If no such space exists, no agreement is possible. The Chair's job is to find it.

**Global Stocktake (GST)** — A five-yearly review under the Paris Agreement in which all countries collectively assess whether their combined efforts are sufficient to meet the Paris temperature goals. The first GST concluded at COP28 and found a significant ambition gap.

**Transparency / MRV (Measurement, Reporting, Verification)** — The system by which countries report their emissions and track progress against their NDCs. The USA consistently demands that any agreement include a transparency framework that applies equally to all major emitters, including China.

**Carbon leakage** — The risk that climate regulation in one jurisdiction causes emissions-intensive industries to relocate to jurisdictions with weaker rules, offsetting the climate benefit. Used to justify border carbon mechanisms.

**Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)** — A tariff on imports from countries with weaker carbon pricing, designed to prevent carbon leakage and level the playing field. The EU has introduced one; it is politically contested by developing countries who see it as a trade barrier.

**Carbon price** — A charge placed on greenhouse gas emissions to make polluters pay for the climate cost of their emissions. Can take the form of a carbon tax or an emissions trading scheme. Canada's carbon price is one of the world's most ambitious.

**Emissions trading scheme (ETS) / carbon market** — A cap-and-trade system in which a limit (cap) is set on total emissions, and permits to emit are traded between actors. The EU ETS is the world's largest. Article 6 of the Paris Agreement governs international carbon markets.

**Article 6** — The section of the Paris Agreement governing international carbon markets — mechanisms by which countries and companies can trade emission reductions across borders. Highly contested; rules were finalised at COP29 after years of negotiation.

**Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC)** — The right of Indigenous communities to give or withhold consent before projects affecting their lands and resources are approved. Referenced in Canada's mandate in the context of energy infrastructure.

**Social discount rate / pure rate of time preference (ρ)** — The rate at which future welfare is discounted relative to present welfare in economic models. A high discount rate (e.g. Nordhaus: ~1.5%) treats future climate damages as less important than present costs. A low or near-zero discount rate (e.g. Stern: ~0.1%) treats future generations' welfare as nearly equal to present welfare. This parameter encodes a normative assumption about intergenerational equity, not a scientific fact.

### Model and Analysis Terms

**Pareto front / Pareto archive** — The set of solutions produced by the optimisation in which no solution is strictly better than another on all objectives simultaneously. Each point on the front represents a different climate policy — a different trade-off between welfare and emission control stringency. High-ambition solutions sit at one end (lower temperature, higher abatement cost); low-ambition solutions at the other.

**High ambition solution** — A Pareto solution with aggressive emission control rates, lower peak temperature, higher abatement costs, and lower `fraction_of_ensemble_above_threshold` at 1.5°C and 2°C.

**Low ambition solution** — A Pareto solution with modest emission control rates, higher peak temperature, lower abatement costs, and higher `fraction_of_ensemble_above_threshold`.

**Satisficing criterion** — The minimum acceptable outcome an actor defines for their key variable(s). Unlike optimisation (finding the best), satisficing asks: does this solution meet my threshold? Used to select a preferred Pareto solution and to assess robustness.

**Robustness** — The degree to which a preferred policy continues to meet the satisficing criterion across different scenarios and ensemble members. A policy that works under one SSP scenario but fails under others is not robust.

**Ensemble** — A set of model runs that sample uncertainty in climate sensitivity and other parameters. Each ensemble member represents a plausible climate future given the same policy. The spread across ensemble members represents deep uncertainty that cannot be reduced by more data.

**SSP scenarios** — Shared Socioeconomic Pathways — scenarios that describe different possible futures for society (population, economic growth, technology, land use) that drive emission trajectories. JUSTICE uses SSP scenarios 0–7 for robustness testing.

**Welfare function** — The mathematical expression used in JUSTICE to define what counts as a good outcome. The choice of welfare function encodes normative assumptions about whose welfare matters and how much. See Section 1.4.

**Abatement** — The reduction of greenhouse gas emissions. `abatement_cost` in JUSTICE is the economic cost of reducing emissions — the investment in clean energy, efficiency, and technology required to lower emissions from the baseline.

**Backstop technology** — In JUSTICE, the clean technology that can fully replace fossil fuels at a cost (`backstop_cost`). A lower backstop cost means the transition is cheaper. The `backstop_cost` parameter can be varied to represent faster or slower technological progress.

**MOEA (Multi-Objective Evolutionary Algorithm)** — The optimisation algorithm used in JUSTICE to generate the Pareto archive. It searches the policy space to find solutions that cannot be improved on all objectives simultaneously.

### Key Institutions and Groupings

**UNFCCC** — United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change. The 1992 treaty that established the international climate governance framework. All subsequent agreements (Kyoto Protocol, Paris Agreement) sit under it.

**COP (Conference of the Parties)** — The annual meeting of all UNFCCC member states where climate negotiations take place. COP31 is in Antalya, Turkey, in 2026.

**G77 + China** — The largest negotiating bloc in the UNFCCC, representing 134 developing countries plus China. Not ideologically uniform — includes SIDS, LDCs, OPEC members, and major emerging economies — but coordinates on shared positions around equity, finance, and differentiation.

**LMDC (Like-Minded Developing Countries)** — A negotiating group within the G77 that takes a harder line on developed-country obligations and resists binding commitments on developing countries. Includes China, India, Saudi Arabia, and others.

**AOSIS (Alliance of Small Island States)** — A coalition of approximately 44 small island and low-lying coastal states that face existential climate risk. Among the most vocal advocates for 1.5°C and Loss and Damage.

**African Group of Negotiators (AGN)** — The negotiating bloc representing all 54 African countries at the UNFCCC. Coordinates positions on adaptation finance, Loss and Damage, and historical responsibility.

**LDC Group (Least Developed Countries)** — The 44 poorest countries in the world, recognised under the UNFCCC as deserving special support. Coordinate on grant-based finance, adaptation, and the sufficiency floor.

**BASIC** — Brazil, South Africa, India, China — a coalition of major emerging economies that coordinates on resisting binding external targets and defending the principle of differentiation.

**High Ambition Coalition (HAC)** — A cross-regional group of countries pushing for stronger climate commitments. Includes the EU, many island states, and some developing countries. Committed to 1.5°C.

**Annex I / Non-Annex I** — The original UNFCCC classification: Annex I countries (developed, industrialised) were expected to take the lead on emission reductions; Non-Annex I (developing) had fewer obligations. This distinction has become increasingly contested as major emerging economies have grown.
